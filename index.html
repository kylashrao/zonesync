Why time zone differences exist

Earth is divided into roughly 24 time zones, each generally spanning 15 degrees of longitude, so that local clock time stays loosely aligned with the position of the sun. As you travel east, clocks move forward; as you travel west, they move back. This is why a meeting at 9 AM in New York lands at completely different hours in London, Mumbai, or Tokyo.

Many countries also observe daylight saving time (DST), shifting their clocks forward in spring and back in autumn to make better use of daylight hours. Because different countries start and end DST on different dates — and some don't observe it at all — the time difference between two cities can actually change twice a year. ZoneSync accounts for this automatically using each location's official time zone rules, so the conversions you see are always accurate for today's date, not just a fixed offset.

How ZoneSync calculates time differences

Every city page and conversion on this site reads your device's current time and recalculates live using standard IANA time zone identifiers (for example, America/New_York or Asia/Kolkata). This means leap years, DST transitions, and historical time zone changes are all handled correctly without you needing to do any manual math.
